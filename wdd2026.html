Understanding the Diagnosis

Dementia is not
one single disease.

"Dementia" describes a group of symptoms — memory loss, confused thinking, language trouble, mood changes — that disrupt daily life.

Several different diseases can cause those symptoms. Knowing which one matters: treatment, planning, and outlook all depend on it.

That is why screening early gives doctors the most options.

The Main Types

  • 60–80% Alzheimer'sThe most common cause. Memory loss usually first.
  • ~10% VascularLinked to strokes and reduced blood flow.
  • 5–10% Lewy BodyHallucinations and movement issues common.
  • 5–10% FrontotemporalOften earlier onset. Personality changes first.
  • ~50% Parkinson's-relatedOf Parkinson's patients, in later stages.
Early Warning Signs

Eight things worth a second look.

Forgetting one thing is normal. Forgetting often, or losing track of daily life, is a reason to ask.

01

Forgetting things that just happened

Recent talks or plans slip away while older memories stay clear.

02

Poor judgment, out of character

Strange money decisions. Falling for scams. Ignoring hygiene.

03

Things put in odd places

Keys in the freezer. Glasses in the dishwasher. No memory of how.

04

Trouble with familiar tasks

Forgetting steps in a recipe known by heart. Lost on a familiar route.

05

Words that won't come

Stopping mid-sentence. Calling a wristwatch a "hand clock."

06

New mood or personality

Sudden anger, worry, suspicion — or a flat indifference.

07

Confused about time or place

Losing track of seasons or how they got somewhere.

08

Pulling away from people

Dropping hobbies. Avoiding friends and family events.

The Tool We'll Use · AD8

Eight short questions.
About five minutes.

The AD8 is an internationally trusted screening tool. A trained staff member walks you through it, one-on-one, in plain language.

A higher score doesn't diagnose anything by itself. It just tells you whether to follow up with a doctor. Results are confidential.

8questions

"Has there been a change?" That is the heart of the AD8 — designed for a family member, but you can also take it yourself.

Six Habits That Help

You can't control everything.
But you can do a lot.

Roughly 40% of dementia cases are linked to risk factors that respond to lifestyle change (Lancet Commission, 2024). Together, these habits move the odds.

Step 01

Get screened — early

If something has felt off for six months or more, don't wait. Earlier testing means more options.

Step 02

Keep your mind in motion

Read. Play cards. Learn something new. The brain works best with regular use.

Step 03

Move every day

Walking. Tai chi. Dancing. Anything that gets your heart rate up helps the brain.

Goal · 150 minutes a week

Step 04

Eat for your brain

Greens, berries, olive oil, fish twice a week. Less red meat, sugar, and fried food.

Goal · 5+ vegetable servings a day
Step 05

Manage chronic conditions

High blood pressure, diabetes, and high cholesterol all hurt the brain. Take meds. Get checkups.

Blood pressure · under 130/80
Step 06

Sleep well, stay close

Seven to eight hours of real sleep. The brain clears waste while you sleep. Loneliness also raises risk.

Goal · 7–8 hours nightly
